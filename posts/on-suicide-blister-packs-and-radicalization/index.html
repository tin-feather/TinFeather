On Suicide, Blister Packs, and Radicalization

A friend sent me a link to an article repeating the too oft told story of a somewhat directionless single white male turning to a popular video streaming service and getting sucked down an alt-right rabbit hole. This particular individual realized what had happened, changed political agendas, and is participating in a left-leaning counter action. \(\iota\) do not wish to discuss politics either way but instead want to make an observation about medical packaging.

The idea that blister packs for medications could lessen drug-induced suicides appears to have taken hold in 2013. A simple search shows articles, including one from that same source, discussing the simplicity and effectiveness of such a solution. While it is a physically simple act to swallow 30 or 40 pills from a bottle, the deliberation required to push the same number from blister packs has been shown to drastically lower the number of suicide attempts through such means.

What if video streaming services did not have auto-play and such ubiquitous "Up next" suggestions? Would that be enough to lessen the risk of radicalization? The subject of the first article eventually realized how an algorithm fed him increasingly extreme points of view, leading him to conclusions he would not otherwise have reached. Would he have deliberately sought out such material if it had not been presented in the same browser pane? Just one tantalizing, click baity link away?

Counter accusations of nanny-state interventionism arise against the practice of blister packs for dangerous medications. Say what you will of pharmaceutical companies, but we are not yet to the point where medication slips itself into our mouths, and invites us to simply swallow. At least not yet …

\(\iota\) think it is time finally to look into Greasemonkey and start writing some plug-ins.
